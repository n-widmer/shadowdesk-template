# Retain + Reward — keep them, then pay them off

Source: *$100M Leads*, Ch. 6. Hook gets the start; Retain keeps them consuming; Reward satisfies the reason they started. A unit that hooks but doesn't pay off is a failed unit.

---

## Retain — drive curiosity

"My favorite driver of retention is curiosity... People want to know what happens... next." The mechanism: "embed questions in their minds" — explicit or implied. Three ways:

- **Lists** — "things, facts, tips, opinions, ideas, etc. presented one after the other." Good lists follow a theme ("Top 10 Mistakes," "5 Biggest Money Makers"). "Giving the number of listed items in your headline, or in the first few seconds of your content, tells people what to expect. And in my experience, this retains more of the audience's attention for longer." (e.g. "7 Ways I Invested $1000 in My 20s That Paid Off Big," "28 Ways To Stay Poor.")
- **Steps** — "actions that occur in order and accomplish a goal when completed. Provided the early steps were clear and valuable, the person will want to know how to do them all." (e.g. "3 Steps to Creating a Great Hook," "How I Create a Headline in 7 Steps.")
- **Stories** — "describe events, real or imaginary... stories worth telling often have some lesson or takeaway." Things that happened, might happen, or never will — "All three drive curiosity because people want to know what happens next."

**Lists vs. steps:** "Steps are actions that must be done in a specific order to get a result. So steps are less flexible but have a more explicit reward. Lists can have just about anything on them in any order... more flexible but have a less explicit reward."

**Interweave freely:** "you can have lists within steps, and a story about each list item... stories to reinforce the value of a step... a list of stories... Your creativity is the only limit here."

**Action step:** "Use lists, steps, and stories to keep your audience curious. Embed questions in their minds to make them want to know what happens next."

---

## Reward — completely satisfy the hook

"The real question is — is it good? Does it satisfy the reason they watched to begin with? Does it make people want to share it? How good your content is depends on how often it rewards your audience in the time it takes them to consume it. **Think value per second.**"

"There is no such thing as too long, only too boring."

Increase the chance of reward by: hooking the right audience, retaining with lists/steps/stories, and "clearly satisfying the reason the content hooked them to begin with."

**Bad-reward failures (avoid these):**
- Hook promises "7 Ways to Make Up with Your Spouse" and you give four ways, or seven weak/recycled ways, or you're talking to a room of single guys. "You did a bad job of rewarding. People will not want to watch again, and certainly won't share it."
- Hook promises "4 Marketing Strategies Dentists Can Use" and they can't actually use them. Same failure.

> "The audience decides. Rewarding your audience means matching or exceeding their expectations when they decide to consume your content. Here's how you know if you succeeded: your audience grows."

**Action step:** "Provide more value than anyone else. Make good on your promises. Clearly satisfy the hook you used to get their attention. In other words, completely answer the unresolved questions you embedded in their minds."

## Quality guardrails — the seven content lessons (Ch. 7)

Apply these while building Retain and Reward so the unit reads like a person, not a guru:

1. **"How I" not "How To."** "Talk about what you've done, not what others should do. What you like, not this is the best. When you talk about experience, no one can question you. This makes you bulletproof." ("My favorite way to generate leads" beats "This is the best way to generate leads.")
2. **Remind more than teach.** "You're a silly goose if you think 100 percent of your audience listens 100 percent of the time." Repetition is fine; you'll be bored of a point before the audience has even seen it.
3. **Puddles → ponds → lakes → oceans.** "Narrow the focus of your content." Become king of the puddle (what you do, where you do it) before reaching for general business. Specific beats broad early.
4. **Content is a tool for sales.** The best-performing units double as assets a salesperson sends before/after a call — especially ones that resolve a common objection.
5. **Free content retains paying customers.** Good free content makes buyers loyal; bad free content makes them like the paid thing less.
6. **Not shorter attention spans — higher standards.** "There's no such thing as too long, only too boring." Earn the length.
7. **Avoid pre-scheduling.** Manually-posted content performs better because the close feedback loop makes you try harder. (A craft note, not a step in this skill, but it's why the unit should be good enough to post by hand.)

## Short vs. long form

"To create a longer piece of content, we simply link content units together." A single step in a five-step list can itself be a content unit; link five and you have a longer piece. "Shorter content hooks, retains, rewards fewer times. Longer content does it more times" — and stringing more good units in a row takes more skill. Start short, then build.
