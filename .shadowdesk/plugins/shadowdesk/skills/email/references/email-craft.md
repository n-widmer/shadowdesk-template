# Email Craft — universal, voice-agnostic

*Provided as part of your ShadowDesk engagement. Not for resale or redistribution.*

This is the self-contained craft the `/email` skill applies on every draft. It is voice-agnostic: it works for any sender. If a client has their own email voice guide wired up (`voiceGuidePath`), that guide OVERRIDES this file wherever the two conflict (specific openers, sign-offs, phrasings, banned words). Where there's no client guide, this file is the clean, plain, human-sounding default.

---

## 0. The core rule

**Write like you talk. Short, warm, direct. The email transmits one thing; the real conversation happens on calls. The default failure is over-writing: padding to hit a word count, decorating, recapping. If you're padding, stop.**

Corollary: ask first — does this email even need prose? Sometimes the correct output is a login and a signature. No recap. No warm-up. No sign-off.

## 0.5 The brackets are the danger zone

The middle of an email is usually fine: the facts, the asks, the next steps come out right. The problem is almost always the opener and the closer. That's where corny phrasing and bow-wrapping leak in.

**Rule: spend disproportionate care on the first line and the last line.** If either feels like it's trying to sound clever, be warm-in-a-particular-way, or wrap a bow, rewrite it. Default to the plainest opener and the plainest close (often no explicit close at all). The middle can work harder; the brackets should do less.

## 0.6 Lead with what the reader gets

Nobody cares what you do or what you build. They care what they get. The first real line should orient around the reader's benefit, the referral that connects you, or a specific thing you noticed — not "I'm X and I build Y." Save any self-description to one terse line, and only if it earns its place.

## 1. Length: discernment, not a target

No minimum. Length follows content density.

**Count the things. One short paragraph per distinct thing. Plus the warm bracket (opener + close).**

A "thing" = an action item, an unresolved thread, a commitment, a piece of new information, or a specific person-topic promised as follow-up.

| # of things | Target length | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 20–50 words | Handoff, scheduling, a quick confirmation |
| 2–3 | 60–110 words | Standard follow-up (a login + an ask, two intros) |
| 4–5 | 120–180 words | A dense meeting with multiple live threads |
| 6+ | Break it up | Two emails, or a clearly bulleted section. 200+ words is a smell. |

**Two modifiers:**
1. **Relationship stakes.** Someone you want to deepen a relationship with gets one extra warm line. A transactional contact gets none.
2. **The "already heard it" rule (the most important one).** If a thing was thoroughly discussed on the call, it does NOT count as a thing. Only list threads with an actual open question or commitment. If the call covered pricing, features, and schedule, the follow-up email does not re-state pricing, features, and schedule. The email carries the NEW item (the login, the link, the one open question), not a re-performance of the meeting.

**Hard ceilings by type** (sanity check, not a target). If a draft exceeds the ceiling for its type, cut. Don't apologize for length — just cut.

| Type | Typical | Hard ceiling |
|---|---|---|
| Handoff / artifact | ~20 words | 60 words |
| Reply | ~80 words | 150 words |
| First-touch | ~95 words | 180 words |
| Follow-up | ~95 words | 180 words |
| Bump | ~90 words | 130 words |

## 2. Structure by email type

Pick the subsection that matches the Phase 1 situation.

### 2a. Post-meeting follow-up

1. Opener (plain greeting).
2. **One** warm line acknowledging the meeting — a specific detail if there is one, generic if not. Not three. Not a paragraph.
3. The specific thing(s) from the meeting (1–3 items max). Can be a brief list, but only of things with an open question or commitment, never a recap of what was already covered.
4. The ask or next step, with a concrete time or action.
5. Warm close — often just ends on a natural sentence.

### 2b. First-touch / warm intro

1. Opener.
2. One line of context — who referred you, what they said, or a specific thing you noticed. Lead with this, not with yourself.
3. At most one terse line on what you do (no positioning bragging), framed around what the reader gets.
4. The ask — usually "grab coffee," "book time," or "let me know."
5. Short close.

### 2c. Mid-thread reply

No greeting required — the name is already in the thread. Answer the question. If there's a next step, name it. Done. Shortest of all the types.

### 2d. Follow-up bump ("haven't heard back")

A worn-in template. Don't invent a new one each time:
1. Opener (or no greeting).
2. A short line like "Bumping this up, wanted to make sure it didn't get buried."
3. ONE fact or hook they didn't hear before — ideally a stat or a value-add, not a guilt trip.
4. Offer alternatives: "Happy to resend X. Or if it's easier to talk it through, [phone]."
5. Short close.

### 2e. Handoff / artifact (credentials, files, links)

**Often the correct output is 5–30 words and no prose.** If the recipient just spoke to you and agreed to the thing, the email is the transmission, not the pitch. Example: the login URL, the email, the password — and the signature handles the rest. If context argues for one sentence of framing, keep it to exactly one ("Here's your login, poke around whenever"). Not four. Not "recapped for your convenience."

### 2f. Intro (connecting two people)

1. "[Person A], meet [Person B]."
2. One sentence on why each person matters.
3. Hand off: "I'll let you two take it from here."

## 3. Openers — principles (not exact percentages)

Match the opener to the relationship. Most emails want a plain, warm greeting with the first name ("Hey [Name],"). Familiar contacts can take just the first name and a comma. Mid-thread replies usually take no greeting at all because the name is already in the thread. A first or barely-met contact can take a slightly more formal greeting ("Hi [Name],"). The point is plainness — the opener should never be the line where you try to sound clever.

Banned openers (universal): "I hope this finds you well," "I wanted to reach out," "I wanted to see if," "Just wanted to touch base," "Per our conversation," "As discussed," "Following up on...," any metaphor-flex opener ("X is a hell of a way to Y," "X sets the tone," "X was a masterclass in Y").

---

## 4. Sign-offs — principles (not exact percentages)

Most emails are best ending on a natural sentence and letting the signature carry the name. A short close ("Talk soon," "Thanks,") is good when there's a concrete next touch. Don't stack multiple sign-offs. If a signature block is being appended to the draft, do NOT also type the sender's name above it — that reads doubled-up; the signature is the sign-off.

Never use (universal): "Best regards," "Sincerely," "Warm regards," "Looking forward to hearing from you," or a multi-line sign-off stack.

---

## 5. Do NOT recap what the recipient already heard

This is the rule that most separates a human follow-up from an AI one. After a call, the temptation is to "recap for your convenience" — pricing, features, schedule, everything that was just discussed. Don't. The recipient was in the room. The email's job is to transmit the NEW thing (the link, the login, the one open question, the promised intro), not to re-perform the meeting. A bulleted feature-list recapping what was already covered on the call is a hard fail. Walk each paragraph and ask "did they already hear this?" — if yes, cut it.

## 6. Structural micro-rules

- **Paragraphs are short** — often 1–2 sentences. Big blocks of text don't belong in a real person's email. Use real paragraph breaks (a blank line between paragraphs), not a wall.
- **Sentences are short** — most under 20 words. A 40-word sentence is usually two sentences.
- **Exclamation marks** — one or two per email at most, not five. Genuine enthusiasm is fine; multi-exclamation defaults read manic.
- **Emojis** — essentially none in email by default. A single one is acceptable only when it labels a specific thing (a result, a metric) and never as decoration.
- **Em dashes** — none. Use commas, parentheses, or start a new sentence. Wanting an em dash is the signal to break the sentence in two.
- **Links** — friendly hyperlink with short anchor text that names the destination ("watch the recording," "book a call," "the proposal," "your login"). Never drop a long raw or tokenized URL into the body. A short vanity URL as plain text is fine.
- **Names** — the recipient's name usually appears once, in the opener. Repeating it two or three times in the body is a tell and reads sycophantic.

## 7. The universal AI-slop blacklist

These phrases and structures read as machine-written. Never emit them (the `/email` skill greps the draft for the word-level ones in Phase 4, and adds the client's own `bannedPhrases` on top).

**Word/phrase level:** em dash (—), double-dash (--), "circling back" / "circle back," "touching base" / "just wanted to touch base," "I hope this finds you well," "per our conversation," "synergy," "game-changer," "deep dive," "excited to explore," "reach out," "at the end of the day," "sets the tone," "masterclass."

**Structure level:**
- Binary contrasts / negation reversals ("Not X. It's Y.") → state the point directly.
- Negative listing ("Not a X. Not a Y. A Z.") → state the thing without the runway.
- False agency ("the decision emerges," "the data tells us") → name the human actor.
- Narrator-from-a-distance ("Nobody designed this," "People tend to...") → use direct address.
- Passive voice that hides the actor ("mistakes were made") → name who did it.
- Vague declaratives ("the implications are significant," "the stakes are high") → name the specific thing.
- Crutch adverbs (genuinely, fundamentally, inherently, crucially, importantly) → cut them.
- Throat-clearing openers ("here's the thing," "let me be clear," "the truth is," "it turns out") → cut to the point.

---

## 8. Pre-emit gut check

Read the draft aloud in your head. Does it sound like someone warm, direct, slightly hurried, moderately enthusiastic? Or like a LinkedIn post? If it's the latter, rewrite. When in doubt, prefer shorter, warmer, less ceremony.
